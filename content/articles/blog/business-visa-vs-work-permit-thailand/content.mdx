## Do you need both a business visa and a work permit?

**Yes.** In most cases, foreign employees working for a Thai employer need both a **Non-Immigrant B business visa** and a **work permit**.

The business visa is immigration permission to **enter and stay** in Thailand for employment-linked activity with an approved sponsor. The work permit is labour authorization to **perform the work itself** for that sponsor after you arrive. The visa does not replace the permit. The permit does not replace the visa.

You need the business visa (or equivalent long-stay category) to enter on the correct status. You need the work permit before employment begins. Holding one without the other does not complete legal authorization for a typical Thai job.

Exceptions are narrow. Some roles may qualify for specific exemptions, and short business visits without local employment follow different routes. If you are not taking a Thai payroll job, compare other visa categories before you file a Non-Immigrant B.

## What is a business visa?

A Thailand **business visa** is usually a **Non-Immigrant B** visa issued at a Thai embassy or consulate abroad. It is immigration permission to enter and stay for employment-linked or company-linked activity with an approved sponsor.

It answers whether you may enter Thailand on a long-stay category tied to that sponsor. It does **not** authorize you to perform work in Thailand.

For employees, the file is commonly linked to a **WP3** (preliminary labour approval from the Ministry of Labour) plus employer documents such as company registration, tax filings, and an employment offer letter. Embassy checklists vary by nationality and processing post.

## What is a work permit?

A **work permit** is issued by the **Department of Employment** (Ministry of Labour). It authorizes a named foreign national to perform a **specific job** for a **specific Thai employer** in Thailand.

It answers whether you may legally perform that work. Thai law treats working without a valid permit as a serious compliance breach, even if you already hold a business visa.

Typical files include your passport and visa status, company registration and tax documents from the sponsor, your contract and job description, a medical certificate, and other items per local office practice. The permit is tied to employer, role, and location. Change any of those materially and you usually need a new permit review.

## How this applies in real situations

### Employee hired by a Bangkok company

You sign with a Thai employer and will work on a local payroll. **You need both documents:** Non-Immigrant B before travel, then a work permit after entry. Do not start onboarding until the permit is approved.

### Company director actively managing operations

You are listed as a director and will run day-to-day work in Thailand. **A business visa plus work permit is the normal path** when you perform work locally. A title alone does not replace labour authorization.

### Overseas employee relocating to Thailand

You live abroad today and will move after visa approval. **Apply for the business visa at the embassy with jurisdiction over your nationality or residence**, enter on that status, then complete the permit with your sponsor. Personal documents must match your processing post checklist.

### Consultant attending meetings only

You will visit for short meetings and will not perform work in Thailand. **A full business visa and work permit file is usually not the right fit.** A business visitor or short-stay route may suffice. Confirm intent before you commit to a sponsor-led employment file.

### Remote worker with no Thai employer

You earn from overseas clients or a foreign employer and will not join a Thai payroll. **You do not need this pair.** Compare [DTV](/visas/dtv) or other long-stay routes on the relevant visa pages instead of forcing a Non-Immigrant B.

## Why are business visas and work permits confused?

**Both documents appear in the same employment story, but they are issued by different agencies for different purposes.** Employers, forums, and even some service providers use "work visa" as shorthand for the whole process.

**Four reasons the mix-up persists:**

1. **Both are required for most jobs.** Applicants need both in sequence, so people refer to the whole bundle as "getting a work visa."
2. **Different agencies, one workflow.** Immigration handles the visa. Labour handles the permit. No single government form completes both steps.
3. **Sponsors vary in readiness.** Some companies understand WP3 and company packs. Others assume the employee can "sort the visa" alone.
4. **Official labels sound like permission to work.** Non-Immigrant B categories are often described as "Business and Work," which suggests the visa itself grants work rights. It does not.

## Decision framework

**Use this framework when you are deciding whether a business visa and work permit are your route** before you prepare documents or book flights.

**You likely need a business visa first (then a work permit) when:**

- A Thai company will employ you in Thailand
- You are a director or manager who will **perform work** in Thailand, not only hold shares passively
- You are joining a BOI-promoted company with a defined role in Thailand
- Your stay intent is tied to a sponsor entity, not remote work for foreign clients only

**A business visa and work permit are usually not your answer when:**

- You only need short business meetings and will not work in Thailand
- You work remotely for foreign income with no Thai employer (compare [DTV](/visas/dtv) or other routes)
- You are retired or financially independent without Thai employment (compare [Thailand Retirement Visa](/visas/retirement) or [Thailand Elite Visa](/visas/elite) pages)

**You need professional guidance early when:**

- Your sponsor is newly registered or has never hired foreign staff
- You are unsure whether WP3 is required at your embassy
- You hold another visa type and hope to "switch" inside Thailand without leaving
- You are a director-shareholder with mixed passive and active duties

## Common mistakes

**Most compliance problems come from treating the visa as permission to work, or the permit as a substitute for the correct visa category.**

### Treating visa approval as permission to start work

Some applicants assume embassy approval means they can begin on day one. **Starting work before the work permit is issued** creates compliance risk for you and your sponsor. Plan onboarding dates after permit approval.

### Expecting to file the work permit alone

The work permit is sponsor-led. **Employees who prepare only personal documents** without employer coordination often stall at step one. Confirm your company's HR or legal contact owns the labour filing.

### Using tourist or visa-exempt entry to "sort it later"

Arriving without the correct long-stay category and hoping to convert in-country is a frequent failure pattern. **Immigration status and labour authorization are linked.** Get the route right before entry when possible.

### Confusing director title with automatic work rights

Being listed as a director does not remove permit requirements when you actively manage the business in Thailand. **Titles do not replace labour authorization.**

### Copying forum advice about WP3 and embassy packs

WP3 requirements, company document formats, and financial evidence vary by embassy. **A file that worked for another nationality or post may fail for yours.** Use your processing post checklist and the [Thailand Business Visa](/visas/business) page as anchors.
