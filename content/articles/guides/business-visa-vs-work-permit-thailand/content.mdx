## What is a business visa

A Thailand **business visa** is usually issued as a **Non-Immigrant B** visa at a Thai embassy or consulate abroad. It is immigration permission to enter Thailand for employment-linked or company-linked activity with an approved sponsor.

The business visa answers three practical questions for immigration officers:

- Who is sponsoring your stay?
- What is the purpose of your long-stay permission?
- Do you meet the embassy's financial and document standards for that sponsor?

It does **not** answer whether you may perform work in Thailand. That is the work permit's job.

For employees, the visa is commonly tied to a **WP3** or equivalent preliminary approval from the Ministry of Labour, plus employer documents such as company registration, shareholder lists, and an employment offer letter. Embassy checklists vary by post and nationality.

## What is a work permit

A **work permit** is issued by the **Department of Employment** (Ministry of Labour). It authorizes a named foreign national to perform a **specific job** for a **specific Thai employer** in Thailand.

Where the business visa is about entry and stay eligibility, the work permit is about **labour authorization**. Thai law treats working without a valid permit as a serious compliance breach, even if you hold a long-stay visa.

Work permit applications typically require:

- Your passport and current visa status
- Company documents from the sponsor (registration, tax filings, employee ratios where applicable)
- Your employment contract and job description
- Medical certificate and other items per local office practice

The permit is not a generic "work in Thailand" card. It is tied to employer, role, and location. Change any of those materially and you usually need a new permit review.

## Why they are commonly confused

The confusion is understandable because both documents appear in the same employment story. Employers, forums, and even some service providers use "work visa" as shorthand for the whole process.

**Four reasons the mix-up persists:**

1. **Both are required for most jobs.** Because applicants need both documents in sequence, people refer to the whole bundle as "getting a work visa."
2. **Different agencies, one workflow.** Immigration handles the visa. Labour handles the permit. No single form says "complete employment authorization."
3. **Sponsors vary in readiness.** Some companies understand WP3 and company packs. Others assume the employee can "sort the visa" alone. That gap creates partial filings.
4. **Visa labels sound like permission to work.** "Business and Work" appears on official visa categories, which sounds like the visa itself grants work rights. It does not.

<ArticleCallout variant="tip" title="Plain-language rule">
  Think of the **business visa as your entry ticket** linked to a sponsor, and the **work permit as your job license** inside Thailand. You need the ticket to arrive on the right status. You need the license before you work.
</ArticleCallout>

## Decision framework

Use this framework before you prepare documents or book flights.

**You likely need a business visa first (then a work permit) when:**

- A Thai company will employ you in Thailand
- You are a director or manager who will **perform work** in Thailand, not only hold shares passively
- You are joining a BOI-promoted company with a defined role in Thailand
- Your stay intent is tied to a sponsor entity, not remote work for foreign clients only

**A business visa alone is not your answer when:**

- You only need short business meetings and will not work in Thailand (a business visitor route may suffice)
- You work remotely for foreign income with no Thai employer (compare [DTV](/visas/dtv) or other routes)
- You are retired or financially independent without Thai employment (compare [retirement](/visas/retirement) or [elite](/visas/elite) hubs)

**You need professional guidance early when:**

- Your sponsor is newly registered or has never hired foreign staff
- You are unsure whether WP3 is required at your embassy
- You hold another visa type and hope to "switch" inside Thailand without leaving
- You are a director-shareholder with mixed passive and active duties

## Common mistakes

### Treating visa approval as permission to start work

Some applicants assume embassy approval means they can begin on day one. **Starting work before the work permit is issued** creates compliance risk for you and your sponsor. Plan onboarding dates after permit approval.

### Expecting to file the work permit alone

The work permit is sponsor-led. **Employees who prepare only personal documents** without employer coordination often stall at step one. Confirm your company's HR or legal contact owns the labour filing.

### Using tourist or visa-exempt entry to "sort it later"

Arriving without the correct long-stay category and hoping to convert in-country is a frequent failure pattern. **Immigration status and labour authorization are linked.** Get the route right before entry when possible.

### Confusing director title with automatic work rights

Being listed as a director does not remove permit requirements when you actively manage the business in Thailand. **Titles do not replace labour authorization.**

### Copying forum advice about WP3 and embassy packs

WP3 requirements, company document formats, and financial evidence vary by embassy. **A file that worked for another nationality or post may fail for yours.** Use your processing post checklist and the [business visa hub](/visas/business) as anchors.

<ArticleCallout variant="warning" title="Compliance reminder">
  Working in Thailand without a valid work permit can lead to fines, permit cancellation, and visa complications. If you are already in Thailand on the wrong status, get a case review before you start a role or sign an employment contract.
</ArticleCallout>
